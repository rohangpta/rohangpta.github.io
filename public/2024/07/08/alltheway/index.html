Halfway There Pt. 2

July 08, 2024 • personal

Writing this blog post marks the first time I’ve opened up Emacs in a long time. It feels fitting that an end of college post is where I start - but be warned there’s a lot of excessively massaged thought about to spill out in the form of rusty writing.

College is primarily a social experience #

Here’s a few interesting phenomenon I observed about myself:

  • I’m the weighted average of my closest friends. My interests, sense of humour, lifestyle, and views are strongly all influenced by the company I keep - whether I like it or not.
  • The marginal value add of any of my friends has been far greater than that of any class I have ever taken.
  • The marginal value add of ~10 people I met in college has been about 80% the value add of college itself.

An older person recently remarked to me that his college message group is the most active group he’s a part of. College friendships are sticky!

Why? Incentives are pure in college. People aren’t looking to just shake your hand, sign a paper, and make some money. Everyone’s thrown into a chaotic, mixed bag environment; most are simply trying to survive - and so they’re just looking for companionship.

  • This works, because in college:
  • you’re the freest you ever will be
  • you’re the most likely to have a fresh start
  • you’re the most likely to meet your lifelong friends (and partner!)

Your brain is simply the most plastic it will ever be. And forming long lasting social relationships is highly contingent on this plasticity.

So, how would you maximise your dwindling years of heightened neuroplasticity? I like to think the answer is working backward - find the ‘average’ you want to be, and then find company that averages out to that value.
